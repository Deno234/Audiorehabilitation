Generating Speech Material for Auditory Training 
Exercises Using ChatGPT Chatbot
Andrea Andrijašević, Bernard Vukelić
University of Applied Sciences of Rijeka, Rijeka, Croatia
bernard.vukelic@veleri.hr
Abstract — Hearing rehabilitation is a complex process 
comprised of various types of interventions. One of them,  
auditory training, is commonly offered to adults with hearing 
loss that either underwent coch lear implant surgery or adapt 
with difficulty to the prescribed behind-t he-ear hearing aid.
During this type of interventi on, speech material saturated 
with phonemes of different spectral characteristics is 
presented to the patient. In this paper, text analysis and 
generation capabilities offered by the OpenAI’s state-of-the-
art GPT-4 model-based ChatGPT chatbot were assessed
under a set of explicitly stated Croatian language rules and 
strict phonetic criteria, with the final objective of novel 
speech material generation. Th e results indica te that the 
chatbot can successfully genera te isolated words of standard
Croatian language for two target phoneme classes at
saturation levels up to 80 %, and for the remaining three
classes at levels up to 60 %. Moreover, as much as 90 % of 
sentences that it generated at the 50 % saturation level satisfy
the stated phonetic criteria and, even though generally void 
of meaning, adhere to the Croatian language rules and, thus, 
can be used for auditory training exercises.
Keywords – hearing rehabilitation; auditory training;
behind-the-ear hearing ai d; phonemes; chatGPT; chatbot 
evaluation
I. I NTRODUCTION
According to the Andrija Štampar Teaching Institute of 
Public Health report, there were 17 679 persons with 
hearing loss registered in the Croatian Disabilities Registry
in 2023 [1]. As the Registry only contains data for the 
persons with disabling, i.e. severe hearing loss, it has been
estimated that the total number of hearing impaired persons
in Croatia is significantly higher [2]. Hearing loss is a 
global issue, with the World Health Org anization’s
projection that by 2050 almost every fourth person in the 
world will have been affected by it [3]. Due to its main 
symptom – a decrease in speech intelligibility, it renders 
socializing more difficult and, thus, often negatively affects
the psychological state of hearing impaired individuals,
consequently reducing their overall quality of life [3], [4].
Hearing rehabilitation is a long and complex process 
comprised of various types of interventions [4] - [6]. One 
of them, auditory training, is commonly offered to adults 
with hearing loss that either underwent cochlear implant 
surgery or adapt with difficulty to the prescribed behind-
the-ear hearing aid. In the Clinical Hospital Center Rije ka, 
this type of intervention is based on the verbotonal method,
and comprises a set of auditory exercises utili zing speech 
material whose complexity gradually increases [6]. During 
the intermediate phases of auditory training, speech 
material consisting of isolated words and sentences 
saturated with phonemes of specific spectral characteristics
gets presented to the patient, whose tas k is to correctly 
identify it.
Since the tas k of novel speech material generation that 
adheres to the required saturation level as well to the syntax 
and vocabulary of a language is quite challenging for 
humans, in this study, the potential of the OpenAI’s 
ChatGPT chatbot [7], [8] for the text analysis and 
generation tasks, under a set of explicitly stated language 
rules and strict phonetic criteria, was assessed.
An online literature search for relevant publications was 
performed using Scopus, Web of Science (WoS), and
PubMed databases, querying them for topics (llm OR
chatbot OR chatgpt) AND (auditory OR hearing). These
database search strings returned no relevant research papers
on the subject of text generation for auditory training
exercises. A patent, to some extent related to the present
research topic, was identified on WoS – regarding chatbot-
assisted hearing rehabilitation in a question-and-answer
form based on an audio quiz with predetermined voice 
data, as well as the corresponding approved interventional
study [9]. Several opinion piece articles on the possible 
future directions that the development of artificial 
intelligence-assisted hearing rehabilitation could ta ke were 
also identified [10], [11].
The remainder of the paper is structured as follows. In 
Section 2, the methodology utilized for the text analysis
and generation experiments performed with the ChatGPT
chatbot is outlined. The results are presented and discussed 
in Section 3. Finally, Section 4 provides a conclusion to the 
research findings and discusses future work.
II. M ETHODOLOGY
The experiments were performed between November
2023 and January 2024, utilizing the commercial version of 
the OpenAI’s ChatGPT chatbot – the ChatGPT Plus, based 
on the GPT-4 large language model. In the first experiment,
ChatGPT’s text analysis capabilities were evaluated,
whereas in the latter two experiments it was its text 
generation potential that w as under scrutiny. All three
experiments were performed by first presenting the chatbot
with a table of Croatian phonemes arranged in five classes
based on their spectral content, as defined in [12], [13]. The
classification in [13] was slightly modified by removing the
duplicate phonemes (namely, the phonemes /m/, /n/, /nj/,
and /lj/) from the table in the Srednji (Mid) and

A. Experiment 1: Text Analysis
The objective of this experiment was evaluation of
ChatGPT’s ability to analyze sentences in Croatian 
language. It was comprised of four tasks:
(i.) parsing a sentence into phonemes,
(ii.) assigning each phoneme a phoneme class,
(iii.) counting the total number of phonemes in the 
sentence, as well as the number of phonemes of 
each class,
(iv.) calculating the phoneme class distribution of the 
sentence (in percentages).
The first 
two tasks served to evaluate ChatGPT’s ability 
to utilize the table of phoneme classes, whereas the latter 
two tested its elementary mathematical abilities needed in 
order to satisfy the saturation level
set by the user.
Redundancy present in the tas ks (iii.) and (iv.) was 
intentional, so as to evaluate the stability of chatbot’s
output.
The text to be presented to the chatbot comprised of 30 
three to five-words-long sentences saturated with
phonemes of one, i.e. target class, with six sentences per 
class. The sentences were previously utilized in the
computer program Audio exercises described in [6] and
analyzed in [13]. Initial tests revealed ChatGPT’s inability
to correctly par
se text containing phonemes /nj/, /lj/, and
/dž/, mista king them for /n/+/j/, /l/+/j/, and /d/+/ž/ pairs,
respectively. This type of error was not ta ken into account
in the Results section, as we found it reasonable.
The aforementioned four tas ks were integrated into a
prompt of the following form:
Kakvu distribuciju klasa fonema ima rečenica "ždral leti 
daleko"? Od koliko fonema se sastoji ta rečenica?
Izra
čunaj za tu rečenicu ukupan broj fonema za svaku 
klasu fonema uzimajući u obzir ponavljanja pojedinih 
fonema. Izračunaj za tu rečenicu postotke korištenih klasa 
fonema uzimajući u obzir ponavljanja pojedinih fonema.
(What distribution of phoneme classes does the sentence 
„ždral leti daleko“ have? How many phonemes does that
sentence consist of? Calculate for that
sentence the total 
number of phonemes of each phoneme class while ta king 
into account the individual phoneme repetitions. Calculate 
for that sentence the percentages of used phoneme classes 
while ta king into account the individual phoneme
repetitions.)
B. Experiments 2 and 3: Text Generation
The latter two experiments assessed ChatGPT’s text 
generation capabilities. The following three strict language 
and phonetic criteria were embedded in user’s prompts:
(i.) generated text needs to conform to the required
saturation level (SL), i.e. the minimum percentage 
of phonemes of the target class,
(ii.) generated text needs to adhere to the rules of 
standard Croatian language,
(iii.) generated text can be comprised only of words 
present on the https://hjp.znanje.hr/ web page –
Hrvatski jezični portal (HJP), an online dictionary
of standard Croatian language.
In the first text generation experiment, for each of the
five classes, ChatGPT was asked to generate a list of eleven
words that satisfied all aforementioned criteria. In prompts, 
for each class, the saturation level was initially set to 40 %
and then increased to 80 % in 10 % steps. These five
saturation levels were utilized as the majority of isolated 
words used in [6] and [13] are saturated with phonemes
within the 40-80 % range. In order to evaluate the stability 
of its output, for the High class , the chatbot was also asked 
to generate lists of eleven words with the saturation level
decreasing from 80 % to 40 % (denoted as the H2 case).
The aforementioned three criteria were integrated into a
prompt of the following form:
Odaberi i napiši postojeću r iječ iz hrvatskog standardnog 
jezika koja sadrži minimalno 80% fonema koji pripadaju 
klasi Srednji (S ), a preostali fonemi mogu biti iz drugih
klasa fonema. Ta riječ mora biti iz hrvatskog standardnog 
jezika. Provjeri postoji li ta riječ na mrežnoj stranici 
https://hjp.znanje.hr/.
(Choose and write an existing word from standard Croatian 
language that contains minimally 80 % of phonemes that 
belong to the Mid (
M) class, while the remaining phonemes 
can be from other phoneme classes. That word must belong 
to standard Croatian language. Verify whether that word 
exists on the https://hjp.znanje.hr/ web page.)
In the second text generation experiment, for each of 
the five classes, the chatbot was as ked to generate a list of 
twenty-one sentences comprised of three to five words.
Two saturation levels were utilized – 50 % and 70 %, as the 
majority of sentences used in [6] and [13] (and, thus, in 
experiment 1) are saturated with phonemes within this 
range.
At the 50 % SL only the first two criteria were
integrated into prompts, since the results of initial tests 
indicated that at this saturation level ChatGPT outputs
sentences comprised of words of standard Croatian 
language. Unfortunately, this was not the ca
se at 70 % SL
for which the chatbot outputted sentences comprised 
mostly of nonsense words: “mumi mumi muminje mumi”,
“mumija mnom mumi mumi”, “mumin numen pominje”.
Hence, the
third criterion needed to be added into prompts.

III. R ESULTS
A. Experiment 1
The ChatGPT chatbot performed the tas ks (i.) and (ii.)
without a single error – it correctly parsed each of the 30
sentences and correctly classified each of the 432
phonemes they contained. The redundancy present in tas ks
(iii.) and (iv.) enabled identification of two minor issues
regarding its counting abilities.
The first identified
issue was with counting of the total 
number of phonemes in a sentence. This type of error 
occurred only for the five sentences of the Low class, for 
which
the st yle of chatbot’s output differed from the one
for the remaining four classes. For those five sentences, the
total number of phonemes that the chatbot reported and the
total number of phonemes it
used for the calculation of 
percentages differed by one, being correct in the latter case.
The data in Table I illustrate the second identified issue,
reporting the number of sentences for which the chatbot 
correctly counted the number of target class phonemes.
Although the results are far from impressive for the Low-
mid and Mid classes, as in the previously identified 
counting
issue, in each sentence it made a counting error,
the chatbot undercounted the target class phonemes only by 
one.
Perhaps unsurprisingly, ChatGPT di
splayed no issues 
with percentages calculation since it implemented it with
straightforward custom Python code with variable names in
Croatian language. To illustrate this, we present a code

B. Experiments 2 and 3 – Text Length
Figs. 1 and 2 present the distributions of the total number 
of phonemes in generated words and sentences,
respectively, by target class and saturation level criteria.
The generated words have an average length of 6
phonemes, whereas the average length of sentences is 16.5 
phonemes. It can be observed that the median length of
both types of generated text changes only slightly across
saturation levels (one phoneme for words, and one or
two 
phonemes for sentences) for all classes except for the Low-
mid class, for which, interestingly, it changes in opposite 
directions for words and sentences.
 
C. Experiments 2 and 3 – Phoneme Usage
Fig. 3 presents the distri butions of the average 
phoneme usage frequency in generated text, for words and 
sentences separately. The dist ributions were obtained by 
first summing the phoneme usage frequency distributions
of individual words / sentences, and then dividing the 
resulting frequency distributions by the grand total number 
of phonemes in words / sentences. It needs to be 
mentioned that for words, the results obtained for the H1 
and H2 cases were first averaged in order to ensure equal
weighting across the five phoneme classes.
It can be observed that, in general, the distributions for 
words and sentences are of similar shape. Moreover, the 
two most frequently utilized phonemes were the vowels /a/
and /i/
, whereas t he only phoneme that ChatGPT did not 
utilize in text generation was /dž/. The latter result cannot 
be considered surprising, as the phoneme in question is 
rarely used in Croatian language. The phonemes that the 
chatbot used least frequently were /h/, /lj/, /f/, /ž/, and /đ/,
most of which are also quite r are in standard Croatian 
language. Also unsurprisingly, in each class, the most 
frequently utilized phoneme was the vowel the class
contains.

E. Experiments 2 and 3 –
Phonetic Content Dissimilarity
Figs. 6 and 7 present the phonetic content dissimilarity 
(PCD) matrices for the pairs of generated words and 
sentences, respectively. This distance measure is defined as 
the ratio of the number of phonemes that the longer word
/
sentence does not share with the shorter one to the length 
(total number of phonemes) of the longer word / sentence
[13]. In case they are of equal length, PCD is calculated
based on the second word / sentence’s data. An example to
illustrate its use, with the pair of words “jedan” (one) and 
“nedjelja” (Sunday) – the latter, longer word contains 7 
phonemes, of which one /e/ is not shared with the first 
word, as well as the phoneme /lj/, resulting in a PCD of 
0.286.
In Fig. 6, the PCD values are lower within the target
class boundaries, i.e. intra-class, indicating higher 
similarity. The highest simil
arity overall can be observed
among the H1 case generated words, whereas for the H2
case ChatGPT utilized a significant proportion of
phonemes from other classes (also visible in Fig. 4). High
level of intra-
class similarity can be also observed among
the generated sentences in Fig. 7.
F. Discussion
The results of the first experiment indicate that the 
ChatGPT chatbot can successfully parse short sentences in 
Croatian language as well as classify the Croatian
language phonemes. The chatbot is also able to calculate 
percentages faultlessly, while ma king insignificant
counting errors. The results of this text analysis 
experiment suggest the existe nce of necessary abilities in
the chatbot’s GPT-4
large language model needed for 
prompt comprehension and successful performance in text 
generation tas ks. This was confirmed with the results of 
the experiments 2 and 3, in which the chatbot generated
words and sentences of sufficient length, a large 
proportion of which satisfied both the target class 
saturation level criterion and Croatian language rules.

IV. C ONCLUSION
In this paper, a novel method for speech material 
analysis and generation in the context of hearing
rehabilitation that is based on the OpenAI’s ChatGPT 
chatbot was proposed. The results show that the chatbot is
able to generate both words and sentences of sufficient 
l
ength. Words of standard Croatian language were 
successfully generated for the Low and Mid target classes
up to the 80 % saturation level, while very good results
were obtaine d for the remaining three target phoneme 
classes for saturation levels up to 60 %. Moreover, since
nonsense words, i.e. pseudowords are also commonly used
as speech material in
hearing rehabilitation, the chatbot-
generated ones that satisfy the saturation level requested by 
hearing therapists can also be utilized. Furthermore, as
much as 90 % of sentences that ChatGPT
generated at the 
50 % saturation level satisfy the stated phonetic criteria 
and, even though generally void of meaning, adhere to the 
Croatian language rules and, thus, can be used for auditory
training exercises.
As the results of this preliminary study have shown that
the ChatGPT chatbot can efficiently
generate text in 
Croatian language that satisfies both strict language rules
and phonetic criteria, future wor k will be directed towards 
creating a comprehensive dataset of isolated words and 
sentences to be used by hearing therapists in the Clinical 
Hospital Centre Rijeka during rehabilitation of persons who
either underwent cochlear implant surgery or adapt with 
difficulty to the prescribed hearing aid.
